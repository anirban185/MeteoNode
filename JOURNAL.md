# 5/11/2026 6 PM - Schematic Design

_Time spent: 3.25h_

The objective for today’s session was to translate the BOM into a functional schematic. I initially spent time looking for dedicated footprints or integrated library models for the specific modules I'm using (TP5000, AHT20/BMP280 break-outs), but found none that were reliable. I decided to follow the standard modular design approach: using header connectors and precise pin labeling to represent the modules. I placed the ESP32-WROOM-32U as the core, followed by 2-pin connectors for the LiFePO4 battery and solar input, and 4-pin I2C rails for the sensors.

The layout phase was iterative and tedious. Labeling the 4-pin I2C sensors was straightforward, but the high-pin-count connectors for the ESP32 and power management required multiple orientation changes to minimize cross-over. Routing became the bottleneck; as more wires were involved, the "ratsnest" grew messy. I had to re-route several traces to maintain a clean logic flow and ensure the safety systems were properly isolated.

A key addition today was integrating the TP5000’s status pins. The module has pins for external LEDs (Charging/Standby). I wired these to the ESP32's GPIOs so I can monitor the charge state remotely via software. However, the biggest challenge was the remote power-down requirement. I designed a soft-latching power circuit using a high-side P-channel MOSFET as the main switch, controlled by a small N-channel MOSFET. This allows the ESP32 to "hold" its own power on after a button press and programmatically "suicide" (cut its own power) when a shutdown command is received. I also added a 100k-100k voltage divider on the battery rail, tied to an ADC pin, to monitor the LiFePO4’s discharge curve.

i am writing this today cause the 2 hour session yesterday ran too late and i went to sleep before documenting it. yesterday's task was selecting parts for the project. the session took a full 2 hours cause i had to read datasheets to make sure the components actually work together. first i needed a sensor for temperature, humidity, and pressure. my first pick was the bme280, but research showed a flaw: the chip's internal heating messes with its own environmental readings. to fix this, i picked an aht20 and a bmp280. this way i can place the sensors at a distance from each other so their heat doesn't interfere.
next i needed solar panels that output about 2 watts. robu didn't have the mini panels, so i had to search makerbazar to find 6v panels. this is where the charging module became critical. my go-to is the tp4056, but solar panels don't output a stable voltage; they fluctuate wildly based on sunlight and load. the tp4056 requires a strict 5v input to work reliably. i looked for alternatives and picked the tp5000. it is a charging module that has a buck converter circuit with an input range of 4.5v to 9v. this switching architecture handles the unpredictable solar voltage fluctuations perfectly without needing an extra stabilization circuit. i also added 1n5819 schottky diodes to stop the battery from discharging back into the panels at night.
the last part of yesterday's session was power storage. i was going to use classic 18650 li-ion cells, but this node will be outdoors in the sun unsupervised. standard li-ion is risky in high heat and can go into explosive thermal runaway. i looked into safer chemistries and chose lifepo4. it requires temperatures over 270°c for thermal runaway, and because it doesn't release internal oxygen, it won't sustain a fire even if ignited. i selected huaxing’s 32700 solar grade lifepo4 cell from makerbazar and ended the session.
